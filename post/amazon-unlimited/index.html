Amazon Drops Unlimited Storage

Amazon Cloud was the buzz for about 2 years. Launched in 2015, this plan offered unlimited storage for $60 USD/year; making it the cheapest cloud storage plan on the internet.

That’s correct, Amazon had advertised unlimited storage; and at such an affordable price, many peopled had signed up and began using this amazing service.

A large fraction of these users had used this service to upload encrypted “Linux ISOs” at alarming rates. Some users had managed to a petabytes worth of material.

You probably are thinking that this is a really bad idea. In fact, it was. The cost storage is around $50/TB; meaning that $60/year barely covers a single user uploading a PB of data, much less several thousands of users uploading hundreds of TB. And so, as you can imagine, many users got an email from Amazon announcing the closure of their unlimited storage plan.

Now Amazon did offer a way out of this, they reworked their pricing such that it is now $50/year per TB. Which is ridiculous, but still cheaper than Dropbox and Google Drive. However, Apple does offer 2 TB for the same price. And at that price, it makes more sense to just buy a TB hard drive every year.

Amazon Cloud wasn’t the best storage service to begin with. It has very few features in comparison to Google Drive and Dropbox. For example, you can’t edit documents online, easily share folders between friends, and many times the website interface would crash and was unable to load. Not to mention the desktop client is really a piece of shit, So what person in their right mind will choose to opt for such a deal?

While this comes at no surprise, there are a few lessons to learn about this:

  • Marketing should realise that if they use the term “unlimited”, they better hell expect to deal with literally unlimited amounts of data
  • Users should have seen this coming, it’s impossible for a company to sustainably offer unlimited cloud storage at $50/year; even a company like Amazon cannot achieve this.
  • Amazon has now added a new item to their long list of failed services

At this point, it seems the best solution for users wanting to host and distribute 4K Linux ISOs using Plex, it’s best to opt for a home server of NAS such as QNAP or Synology. I myself have shucked a few 8TB drives and have started migrating my Linux ISOs to my Sinology NAS, and I suggest others follow.

Yes, the word “unlimited” storage is soo tempting, but it really is too good to be true.
